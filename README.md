## Project Overview

This project analyses transactional data from a UK-based online gift retailer to uncover actionable business insights across four areas: sales trends, product performance, customer behaviour, and geographic markets.

The goal was to move beyond descriptive statistics and produce insights that could directly inform business decisions around customer retention, revenue risk, and market strategy. A third dashboard layer provides data-backed recommendations with specific next steps for each finding.

---

## Dataset

**Source:** [UCI Machine Learning Repository -- Online Retail Dataset](https://archive.ics.uci.edu/ml/datasets/online+retail)

**Description:** Transactional data from a UK-based online gift retailer between December 2010 and December 2011.

| Column | Description |
|---|---|
| InvoiceNo | Unique invoice number. Prefix "C" indicates cancellation |
| StockCode | Product code |
| Description | Product name |
| Quantity | Units per transaction |
| InvoiceDate | Date and time of transaction |
| UnitPrice | Price per unit in GBP |
| CustomerID | Unique customer identifier |
| Country | Country of the customer |

**Raw size:** 541,909 rows, 8 columns

**Note:** The 2010 data is incomplete. Several months contain only a single day of transactions and should not be used for trend comparisons. All trend analysis in this project is based on 2011 data.

## Data Cleaning

The raw dataset required significant cleaning before analysis:

- Removed 135,080 rows with missing CustomerID values
- Removed 1,454 rows with missing product descriptions
- Removed 9,288 cancelled transactions (InvoiceNo starting with "C")
- Removed rows where Quantity or UnitPrice were zero or negative
- Converted InvoiceDate from string to datetime
- Converted CustomerID from float to string
- Engineered a Revenue column (Quantity x UnitPrice)

**Cleaned dataset size:** 397,884 rows, 9 columns, zero nulls

## Analysis

### Phase 1 -- Descriptive Analysis

| Question | Method |
|---|---|
| Monthly revenue trend | groupby InvoiceDate by month, sum Revenue |
| Highest and lowest sales months | sort_values on monthly revenue |
| Weekly order volume | groupby by week, nunique on InvoiceNo |
| Top 10 products by revenue | groupby Description, sum Revenue |
| Most cancelled products | filter cancellations, groupby Description |
| Unique customers and average order value | nunique on CustomerID, mean on order totals |
| Top customers by spend | groupby CustomerID, sum Revenue |
| Repeat vs one-time customer revenue | map repeat flag to df, groupby and sum |
| Top international markets | filter out UK, groupby Country, sum Revenue |
| Average order value by country | calculated field in Tableau |

### Phase 2 -- Deeper Analysis

| Question | Method |
|---|---|
| New customer acquisition by month | find first purchase date per customer, groupby month |
| Cohort retention analysis | build customer-month table, calculate cohort index, pivot and divide by cohort size |
| RFM customer segmentation | agg Recency, Frequency, Monetary per customer, apply segment rules |
| Top products in top 5 countries | filter to top 5 countries, groupby Country and Description |
| High revenue single-customer products | filter products with only 1 unique customer |
| 80/20 revenue concentration | sort by Monetary, sum top 20% share |

### Phase 3 -- Data-Backed Recommendations

| Question | Method |
|---|---|
| Product overlap: Top 20% vs Bottom 80% customers | split customers by spend rank, groupby Description and sum Revenue per group |
| Country product opportunity gaps | pivot product sales by country, filter for products with high sales in one country and zero in others |
| At Risk customer last purchases | filter df to At Risk CustomerIDs, groupby Description and sum Revenue |
| One-time customer purchase patterns | filter df to one-time CustomerIDs, groupby Description and sum Revenue |

## Key Findings

**Sales Trends**
- Revenue grew steadily through 2011, peaking in September at £1.09M and November at £1.04M
- New customer acquisition declined from 544 in January to 230 by November 2011 -- a concerning downward trend
- Cohort retention improved consistently through 2011, with later cohorts showing stronger month-on-month retention

**Customer Behaviour**
- 93% of total revenue comes from repeat customers -- the business runs almost entirely on loyalty
- 74.6% of revenue is generated by the top 20% of customers -- significant concentration risk
- RFM segmentation identified 1,216 At Risk customers and 958 Lost customers out of 4,338 total
- Only 712 Champion customers (recent, frequent, high spending) are driving the bulk of revenue
- At Risk customers most recently purchased Regency Cakestand, Party Bunting, Jumbo Bag Red Retrospot and White Hanging Heart T-Light Holder -- key products for win-back campaigns
- One-time customers most commonly buy Regency Cakestand, White Hanging Heart and Party Bunting -- the same gateway products that appear across all customer segments

**Products**
- Paper Craft Little Birdie generated £168,470 in revenue from a single customer -- extreme dependency risk
- Regency Cakestand 3 Tier and Rabbit Night Light are consistent performers across multiple international markets
- Top 20% customers buy Chilli Lights and Medium Ceramic Top Storage Jar which Bottom 80% customers do not -- potential upsell products
- Administrative entries (Postage, Manual, Discount) appear in product revenue data and should be excluded from product-level reporting

**Geography**
- UK accounts for 89% of all transactions
- Top international markets are Netherlands (£285,446), Ireland (£265,546), Germany (£228,867), France (£209,024) and Australia (£138,521)
- Singapore and Netherlands have the highest average order values at over £3,000 per order -- indicating large wholesale buyers
- Significant country product opportunity gaps identified -- 3 Tier Cake Tin Red sells strongly in EIRE (£4,236) but has zero sales in Netherlands; 5 Hook Hanger sells strongly in Netherlands (£2,923) but zero in Australia and EIRE

## Recommendations

**1. Launch a Win-Back Campaign for At Risk Customers**
1,216 customers have not purchased in 2 to 6 months. Analysis shows their most recent purchases were Regency Cakestand, Party Bunting, Jumbo Bag Red Retrospot and White Hanging Heart. A targeted email campaign anchored to these specific products -- "We noticed you haven't ordered recently, here's 10% off your next order" -- could recover a meaningful portion before they move into the Lost category.

**2. Protect Champion Customers with a Loyalty Programme**
712 Champion customers are driving the bulk of repeat revenue. Introducing dedicated account management, early access to new products, or a formal loyalty programme would reduce the risk of losing these high value accounts. Given that 74.6% of revenue sits with the top 20% of customers, losing even a small number of Champions would have a disproportionate revenue impact.

**3. Convert One-Time Customers with Follow-Up Campaigns**
One-time customers most commonly buy Regency Cakestand, White Hanging Heart and Party Bunting as their first and only purchase. A follow-up email 30 days after first purchase featuring complementary products could convert a meaningful share into repeat buyers. Customers who make a second purchase are significantly more likely to become long-term loyal customers.

**4. Target Country Product Opportunity Gaps**
Several products have proven demand in one market but zero sales in others. 3 Tier Cake Tin Red generates £4,236 in EIRE but zero in Netherlands. 5 Hook Hanger generates £2,923 in Netherlands but zero in Australia and EIRE. Country-specific campaigns featuring these products in underperforming markets represent low-risk, high-potential revenue opportunities since the products are already proven sellers elsewhere.

**5. Invest in New Customer Acquisition**
Acquisition declined steadily through 2011. Without new customers entering the pipeline, even strong retention will eventually lead to a shrinking customer base. The business should review marketing channels and set monthly acquisition targets to reverse the downward trend identified in the data.

**6. Reduce Revenue Concentration Risk**
With 74.6% of revenue sitting in 20% of customers, the business is highly vulnerable. Top 20% customers buy products like Chilli Lights and Medium Ceramic Top Storage Jar that Bottom 80% customers don't -- these are potential upsell products to promote to the broader customer base to diversify revenue. Single-customer product dependencies like Paper Craft Little Birdie should be flagged to senior management immediately.

## How to Run

**Note on data files:** The raw dataset is not included in this repository due to file size limitations. Download it directly from the [UCI Machine Learning Repository](https://archive.ics.uci.edu/ml/datasets/online+retail) and place it in the `data/raw/` folder before running the notebook. The cleaned CSV files are generated automatically by running the notebook and do not need to be downloaded separately.

1. Clone the repository
2. Download the raw dataset from the UCI link above and place it in `data/raw/` as `online_retail.csv`
3. Open `notebooks/online_retail_analysis.ipynb` in Google Colab or Jupyter Notebook
4. Run all cells in order -- cleaned CSV files will be saved to `data/cleaned/` automatically
5. Open `dashboard/online_retail_dashboard.twbx` in Tableau Desktop or Tableau Public
